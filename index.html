Question 1
  • What is the distribution of catheter indication amongst males and females?
  • Is there a specific year when each indication peaks or dips noticeably in the data? (changed from: Have any indications for catheters become prevalent in a specific year?)
  • What are the trends of the recorded indications?

I wanted to explore the data concerning their "indication for catheter", which refers to the result from or cause of the patients' cardiac catheterization -- which is a procedure used to diagnose and treat cardiovascular conditions. In the dataset there were four possible indications from a patient's catheter file:

  • acute coronary syndrome: an umbrella term for situations where the blood supplied to the heart muscle is suddenly blocked [1]
  • stable angina(also known as angina pectoris): chest pain or discomfort due to coronary heart disease[2], which is a term for the buildup of plaque in the heart's arteries which can lead to heart attack[3]
  • other
  • the indication is unknown/not entered

Of the four, only two of them are actually a cardiovascular condition; other may indicate a condition that doesn't fall into the previous two categories but is still a known condition.

To answer my question I looked at the record counts of these indications and viewed them by sex, year, and then both, and marked anything I thought was noteworthy as well as showed the trendlines for the record counts by year.

First let's look at the gist of the data:

This bar graph shows the total count of files over all the years recorded in the dataset for each indication for a catheter.

This line graph shows the total (both sexes together) record count of the different catheter indications from 2008 to 2016. Noticeable peaks and dips are shown too.

The black lines are the actual counts, the red dotted lines are linear trendlines.

These graphs are pretty straightforward and were made by graphing the catheter indication and sex/year data against the count of number of records. There were no rows that contained null values.

I found that:

  • Acute coronary syndrome clearly has the highest count for both sexes
  • Females consistently have a lower record count, roughly 1:2 female-to-male ratio in every category
  • In general the number of patients receiving a catheter procedure is increasing until 2016
    • There's a peak-dip-peak pattern between 2012-2015 for acute coronary syndrome
    • You can see a dip in patient records in 2012 for stable angina
    • Records for 'other' conditions dipped in 2013 and then rose until 2015
    • There are very few records whether either the data entries were unknown or not recorded. 2012 has the highest count for unknown data
  • The 'Unknown' category looks like a hump so the trendline is pretty flat
